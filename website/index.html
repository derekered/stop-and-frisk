Stop, Question, Frisk

Over the past few years the “stop-and-frisk” program in NYC has come under significant scrutiny for unlawfully targeting minorities, The city is currently reforming the program. We are interested in determining what the data can tell us about where and how the program can be improved.

Find Out More

The Ask

What attributes increase a citizen's likelihood of being questioned and frisked? How effective is the Stop, Question, and Frisk policy in removing contraband in NYC neighborhoods?

Are there attributes that cause a person to be incorrectly frisked (i.e. frisked without discovering contraband)?


Our hypothesis is that race contributes greatly to a person’s likelihood of being questioned and frisked. We will run a regression to identify what attributes (whether it be ethnicity, time of day, etc.) that might have led to them being improperly frisked.

How We Measured

Constant Records

Every time a police officer stops a person in NYC, the officer is supposed to fill out a form to record the details of the stop.

Record Types

The NYPD reports the stop-and-frisk data in two ways: a paper report released quarterly and an electronic database released annually. The electronic database includes nearly all of the data recorded by a policy officer after a stop.

Record Types

The data include the age and race of the person stopped, if the person was frisked, if there was a weapon or firearm recovered, if physical force was used and the exact location of the stop within the precinct.



We will use the 2014 Stop, Question and Frisk data provided by the NYPD. The data includes 112 features, and over 45,000 records.

Our Model

We will utilize regression to identify different attributes that will lead to an individual being frisked and not discovering any contrabands. We will then create a regression for each precinct and determine if there are differences in attributes that may cause one precinct to incorrectly frisk when compared to another precinct.
